FCA FG21/1 — GUIDANCE FOR FIRMS ON THE FAIR TREATMENT 
OF VULNERABLE CUSTOMERS
Source: FCA Finalised Guidance, FG21/1, February 2021

SECTION 1 — INTRODUCTION AND PURPOSE
The FCA expects firms to treat vulnerable customers fairly 
and to achieve outcomes for vulnerable customers that are 
as good as those for other customers. This guidance sets 
out the FCA's expectations of firms in embedding the fair 
treatment of vulnerable customers.

SECTION 2 — DEFINITION OF VULNERABILITY
A vulnerable customer is someone who, due to their personal 
circumstances, is especially susceptible to harm, particularly 
when a firm is not acting with appropriate levels of care.

Drivers of vulnerability include:
(1) Health — conditions or illnesses that affect ability 
to carry out day-to-day tasks
(2) Life events — major life events such as bereavement, 
job loss or relationship breakdown
(3) Resilience — low ability to withstand financial or 
emotional shocks
(4) Capability — low knowledge of financial matters or 
low confidence in managing finances

SECTION 3 — FCA EXPECTATIONS OF FIRMS
Firms should:
(1) understand the nature and scale of vulnerability among 
their customer base
(2) ensure that staff have the skills and capability to 
recognise and respond to the needs of vulnerable customers
(3) respond to customer needs throughout product design, 
flexible customer service provision and communications
(4) monitor and assess whether they are meeting and 
responding to the needs of vulnerable customers and make 
improvements where this is not happening

SECTION 4 — PRODUCT AND SERVICE DESIGN
Firms must consider the needs of vulnerable customers at 
every stage of the product lifecycle. Products and services 
should not be designed in a way that could cause harm to 
vulnerable customers.

A firm should:
(1) identify the target market for each product or service, 
including whether vulnerable customers are likely to be 
in the target market
(2) consider how the features of products and services may 
affect vulnerable customers differently from other customers
(3) test products and communications with vulnerable 
customers where appropriate

SECTION 5 — CUSTOMER SERVICE
Firms should ensure that customer service staff:
(1) can identify signs of potential vulnerability in 
customers they are dealing with
(2) have the skills to deal sensitively with customers 
showing signs of vulnerability
(3) know how to signpost customers to appropriate 
sources of help and support
(4) are not incentivised in ways that could lead to 
poor outcomes for vulnerable customers

SECTION 6 — COMMUNICATIONS
All communications to customers should be designed to 
meet the needs of vulnerable customers. Firms should:
(1) consider the communication needs of vulnerable customers 
when designing communications
(2) make communications available in alternative formats 
where needed
(3) avoid technical language or jargon that may confuse 
vulnerable customers

SECTION 7 — MONITORING AND EVALUATION
Firms must monitor outcomes for vulnerable customers and 
take action where outcomes are poor. Monitoring should include:
(1) analysis of complaints data segmented by vulnerability 
characteristics where possible
(2) customer satisfaction research that captures the 
experiences of vulnerable customers
(3) regular review of whether products, services and 
communications are meeting the needs of vulnerable customers

SECTION 8 — CONSUMER DUTY INTERACTION
The Consumer Duty introduced in July 2023 builds on and 
strengthens the FCA's expectations around vulnerable customers. 
Under the Consumer Duty, firms must deliver good outcomes for 
all retail customers, including those with characteristics of 
vulnerability. The four outcomes — products and services, 
price and value, consumer understanding, and consumer support 
— all have specific implications for how firms treat 
vulnerable customers.
